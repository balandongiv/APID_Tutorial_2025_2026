\subsection{Pre-class Activities}
\subsubsection{Activity 1: Selecting product development process}
\textbf{Objective:} \\
The objective of this activity is to help you in understanding different types of product development processes and subsequently adapt and tailor the development process according to your chosen project.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Study and become familiar with various types of product development processes, such as:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Technology-push products
        \item Platform products
        \item Process-intensive products.
        \item Customized products
        \item High-risk products
        \item Quick-build products
        \item Product-service systems
        \item Complex systems
    \end{itemize}
    \item Determine which development process closely aligns with your team's chosen product.
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item You should consider the characteristics and requirements of your product and compare them with the information presented in Exhibit 2-4.
        \item This analysis will help you identify the most suitable development process for your project.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Activity 2: Selecting Product Development Process Flows} % if available
\textbf{Objective:} \\
The objective of this activity is to guide you in understanding different types of Development Process Flows (DPS) and subsequently help in selecting DPS that most suitable one for your team's needs in developing the selected product. By understanding these process flows will enable you to effectively plan and execute the product development process.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Familiarize yourself with different types of Development Process Flows:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Generic process
        \item Spiral product development process
        \item Complex system development process
    \end{itemize}
    \item Analyze the characteristics and requirements of your team's selected product.
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Consider factors such as complexity, risk, and flexibility in the development process.
    \end{enumerate}
    \item Evaluate each Development Process Flow in relation to your product's needs and assess how each process flow aligns with the specific requirements and constraints of your team project.
    \item Select the most suitable development process flow, for your team.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Activity 3: Selecting departmental structure} % if available
\textbf{Objective:} \\
The objective of this activity is to provide instructions on understanding the composition of a product development team and the departmental structure during the Rally Point phase. By familiarizing yourself with the interdisciplinary nature of product development and assessing the departmental needs for your project, you will be able to effectively organize and allocate resources within your team.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Familiarize yourself with the interdisciplinary nature of product development, which involve the contributions from various department within a company inclusive 1) marketing and sales, 2) engineering, 3) quality assurance, 4) manufacturing, 5) purchasing, 6) legal, 7) financial, and 8) project management.
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item You can refer to Exhibit 1-2 (Development of an electromechanical product) or Exhibit 2.7 (Tyco International) explore the different departmental structure during the rally point various types of product development projects.
    \end{enumerate}
    \item For your own product development project, assess whether it is necessary to maintain, reduce, or add new departments to this list. Provide a well-reasoned explanation for your chosen approach.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Activity 4: Selecting Organizational structure} % if available
\textbf{Objective:} \\
The objective of this section in the lab manual is to guide you in understanding various organizational structures and selecting the most appropriate one for your specific product development project. By familiarizing yourself with these structures and making an informed decision, you will be able to effectively organize and manage your project team.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Begin by familiarizing yourself with different type of organizational structures which include 1) functional organization, 2) project organization, 3) lightweight organization, or 4) heavyweight project organization.
    \item Determine the most appropriate organizational structure for your specific product development project. Take into account the choice you made in Activity 1 and Activity 2 from Session 2.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{In-class Activities}
\subsubsection{Activity 1: Selection of Product Development Strategy}
\textbf{Objective:} \\
The objective of this activity is to walk your team with the process of reviewing and selecting the appropriate product development process in a group setting. Toward the end, it is expected the team will reach a consensus on the product development process.


\begin{enumerate}
    \item At least, when joining this session, it is safe to say to each member of the team should already have generated multiple ideas related to the process of product development, the flow of the product development process, and the structure of different departments involved.
    \item With this assumption, by considering ideas generated by each of the team member, discuss in group to determine
    
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item The appropriate product development process
        \item The optimal flow of the product development process
        \item The optimal flow of the product development process, and \item The suitable departmental structure.
    \end{enumerate}
    \item Do a proper justification for each of selection and compile the finalized selection and submit your group report on the ITEL Page.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{longtable}{|p{3.2cm}|p{4.2cm}|p{5.4cm}|p{2.5cm}|}
\caption{Types of Product Development Processes with Their Features and Examples} \\
\hline
\textbf{Process Type} & \textbf{Description} & \textbf{Distinct Features} & \textbf{Examples} \\
\hline
Generic (Market-Pull) Products & The team begins with a market opportunity and selects appropriate technologies to meet customer needs. & Process generally includes distinct planning, concept development, system-level design, detail design, testing and refinement, and production ramp-up phases. & Sporting goods, furniture, tools. \\
\hline
Technology-Push Products & The team begins with a new technology, then finds an appropriate market. & Planning phase involves matching technology and market. Concept development assumes a given technology. & Gore-Tex rainwear, Tyvek envelopes. \\
\hline
Platform Products & The team assumes that the new product will be built around an established technological subsystem. & Concept development assumes a proven technology platform. & Consumer electronics, computers, printers. \\
\hline
Process-Intensive Products & Characteristics of the product are highly constrained by the production process. & Either an existing production process must be specified from the start, or both product and process must be developed together from the start. & Snack foods, breakfast cereals, chemicals, semiconductors. \\
\hline
Customized Products & New products are slight variations of existing configurations. & Similarity of projects allows for a streamlined and highly structured development process. & Motors, switches, batteries, containers. \\
\hline
High-Risk Products & Technical or market uncertainties create high risks of failure. & Risks are identified early and tracked throughout the process. Analysis and testing activities take place as early as possible. & Pharmaceuticals, space systems. \\
\hline
Quick-Build Products & Rapid modeling and prototyping enables many design-build-test cycles. & Detail design and testing phases are repeated a number of times until the product is completed or time/budget runs out. & Software, cellular phones. \\
\hline
Product-Service Systems & Products and their associated service elements are developed simultaneously. & Both physical and operational elements are developed, with particular attention to design of the customer experience and the process flow. & Restaurants, software applications, financial services. \\
\hline
Complex Systems & System must be decomposed into several subsystems and many components. & Subsystems and components are developed by many teams working in parallel, followed by system integration and validation. & Airplanes, jet engines, automobiles. \\
\hline
\end{longtable}
